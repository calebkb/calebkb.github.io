Industry Overview:

Closure introduces the concept of a digital wardrobe and seeks to integrate the clothing retail, clothing manufacturing and synthetic fiber production layers of industry into one company, while also adding the layer of synthetic clothing recycling to close the loop in the product lifecycle of a garment. These industries have the NAICS codes 4481, 3152, and 32522 respectively. These industries are involved with the production of synthetic fibers which are manufactured into clothing that is sold in retail stores or online. Within the clothing retail market, the customer facing segment of the three industries and thus the main focus of this overview, has provides many garments available in a limited number of sizes. Some retailers offer options for alterations, but many buy wholesale products that are sold as-is. The market features low concentration with only 31% of the market being considered as major players, with the TJX companies controlling 12.6% of the market. However, a similar market is the Department store market, which has high concentration, with Target controlling 54.6% of market share. Further Industry analysis is detailed in the following table.

What 16: Fractional Ownership

         Fractional ownership describes the sharing of a certain asset class amongst a group of owners. Typically, the asset is capital intensive but only required on an occasional basis. While the customer benefits from the rights as an owner, the entire capital does not have to be provided alone.

         What: Designers receive fractional ownership of automated production equipment, which they can use to produce their garments when they need to, without owning the entire process.

Value 52: Two-Sided Market

         A two-sided market facilitates interactions between multi ple interdependent groups of customers. The value of the  platform increases as more groups or as more individual  members of each group are using it. The two sides usually  come from disparate groups, e.g., businesses and private  interest groups.

         Value: Both customers and designers pay to participate on platform

How 9: Crowd-Sourcing

         The solution of a task or problem is adopted by an anony mous crowd, typically via the Internet. Contributors receive a small reward or have the chance to win a prize if their solution is chosen for production or sale. Customer interac tion and inclusion can foster a positive relationship with a company, and subsequently increase sales and revenue.

         How: How designers are chosen to be on the platformand receive partial ownership

Who 46: Shop-in-Shop

         Instead of opening new branches, a partner is chosen whose branches can profit from integrating the company's offer ings in a way that imitates a small shop within another shop (a win-win situation). The hosting store can benefit from more attracted customers and is able to gain constant reve nue from the hosted shop in the form of rent. The hosted company gains access to cheaper resources such as space, location, or workforce.

         Who: The customer is not only the end user, but the designer who pays to participate on the platform and receive fractional ownership

TRIAL AND ERROR

In this trial and error method, the hunch was that a Performance-Based Contracting model could pose a truly disruptive development in the clothing industry. As no company yet offers a digital wardrobe, innovative pricing models could be adapted to optimize the experience for both designers and customers. The Performance-Based Contracting model is described here by the St. Gallen Business Navigator:

A product's price is not based upon the physical value, but on the performance or valuable outcome it delivers in the form of a service. Performance based contractors are often strongly integrated into the value creation process of their customers. Special expertise and economies of scale result in lower production and maintenance costs of a product, which can be forwarded to the customer. Extreme variants of this model are represented by different operation schemes in which the product remains the property of the company and is operated by it.

The performance based contracting model adapted here is based off the music platform startup Resonate. With Resonate, customers purchase credits and the price they pay increases the more a customer listens to a song until the total amount paid covers the cost to purchase a song. With Resonate, after 9 plays of a song, the user has paid the full price of a download to the artist, whereas it takes hundreds of plays on Spotify to equal one purchase. This model could be implemented based on a pure subscription model, where the subscription price is low if the garments are regularly cycled through, but if a user likes a shirt and keeps it for longer, the price of keeping that shirt increases. In order to make this model more profitable, customers could purchase a base garment to own, which gets redesigned every regular design cycle. This model could reduce the costs for garment production, increase revenues for artists, who can now have more of their designs tried out by more customers, while gaining profits for every sale. FINANCIAL CANVAS


Research Design

Revenue Factor 1: Profit Driven by Number of Customers

Assumption: Profit is driven more by the number of customers than the number of sales, because the retention rate of subscriptions will be high

Hypothesis: if the value of a subscription is valued by a user, then the subscription retention rate will be high, and the revenue from monthly subscriptions will exceed the revenue from sales

Experiment: Website will offer two comparable models to customers at time of purchase. The first will offer the base model and subscription service to cover redesigns and fitting, while the second will offer a user a reimbursement if they send their old garment back. The revenues from A & B will be compared.

Revenue Factor Two: Customers will like paying for how much they like a design

Assumption: Because customers only pay full price for designs they like, customers will try more designs and spend more money than they would if they had purchased the designs at full price.

Hypothesis: If customers can try a design for a reduced price, they will try more designs and spend more money in total than if they were purchasing designs for full price

Experiment: Website will utilize A&B testing to offer garments at a flat design price and then at the comparable trial rate. Revenues from both will be compared.

Cost Factor One: The cost of Remaking and Redesigning Shirts Should be Low

Assumption: Remaking and redesigning garments can be done at a low cost which can be offset by a monthly subscription

Hypothesis: If recycling the fabric of garments reduces net material cost, the cost of remaking and redesigning garments will be low and can be covered by a monthly subscription.

Experiment: Initially, remade garments will be sold from two pools. One made out of recycled garments and one made from completely new resources. The cost per unit of remade garment will be compared.

Cost Factor Two: Processing Recycling Materials can be done at a low cost

Assumption: Because recycling centers pay to process waste and plastic producers pay for raw materials, processing recycled materials so they can be made into new materials will be a profitable endeavor if those revenues outweigh the cost of processing them.

Hypothesis: If the cost of processing recyclables is kept low, then a profit can be made converting recyclables to plastic.
